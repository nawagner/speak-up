## Component Breakdown

### 1. Knowledge of the Books (35 points)

**Excellent (32-35 points)**
- Demonstrates thorough knowledge of all three books
- Can discuss specific scenes, passages, and plot developments
- Understands character motivations and relationships
- Accurately recounts key moments and their significance
- Can compare and contrast the three works

**A Moveable Feast - Key Knowledge:**
- Setting: Paris in the 1920s
- Genre: Memoir of Hemingway's years as struggling writer
- Key figures: F. Scott Fitzgerald, Gertrude Stein, Ezra Pound, Hadley (first wife)
- Major themes: poverty, artistic struggle, the Lost Generation
- Famous cafés: Les Deux Magots, Café de Flore, La Closerie des Lilas
- Writing advice: "All you have to do is write one true sentence"

**A Farewell to Arms - Key Knowledge:**
- Setting: World War I, Italy
- Protagonist: Frederic Henry (American ambulance driver)
- Love interest: Catherine Barkley (British nurse)
- Major events: Battle of Caporetto, desertion, escape to Switzerland
- Ending: Catherine dies in childbirth; baby is stillborn
- Famous line: "The world breaks everyone..."

**The Old Man and the Sea - Key Knowledge:**
- Setting: Cuba, Gulf Stream
- Protagonist: Santiago (elderly Cuban fisherman)
- Plot: 84-day unlucky streak, epic battle with giant marlin
- Symbolism: Marlin, sharks, the sea, lions
- Outcome: Fish destroyed by sharks; only skeleton remains
- Manolin: The boy, Santiago's young apprentice

**Good (28-31 points)**

**Satisfactory (24-27 points)**

**Needs Improvement (20-23 points)**

**Unsatisfactory (0-19 points)**

### 2. Hemingway's Biography and Influences (25 points)

**Excellent (23-25 points)**
- Comprehensive understanding of Hemingway's life and experiences
- Can connect biographical events to his writing
- Understands his writing philosophy and style development
- Knows about his influences and who he influenced
- Familiar with his personal struggles and their impact on work

**Essential Biographical Knowledge:**

**Life Events:**
- Born 1899 in Oak Park, Illinois
- WWI ambulance driver (wounded in Italy, 1918)
- Four marriages (Hadley Richardson, Pauline Pfeiffer, Martha Gellhorn, Mary Welsh)
- Paris in the 1920s with the Lost Generation
- Spanish Civil War correspondent (1930s)
- WWII correspondent and participant
- Lived in Cuba at Finca Vigía
- Struggled with depression and health issues
- Suicide in 1961 (Ketchum, Idaho)

**Writing Career:**
- Nobel Prize in Literature (1954)
- Pulitzer Prize for *The Old Man and the Sea* (1953)
- Pioneered "iceberg theory" (theory of omission)
- Known for spare, economical prose
- Journalist background influenced style

**Key Influences:**
- WWI experiences (trauma, disillusionment)
- Paris literary circle (Stein, Pound, Fitzgerald)
- Journalism training (Kansas City Star)
- Outdoor life (hunting, fishing, bullfighting)
- Spanish culture and Civil War
- Personal losses and relationships

**Good (20-22 points)**
- Good understanding of major life events
- Can make some biographical connections to work
- Knows basic writing philosophy
- Familiar with key influences
- Some gaps in detail

**Satisfactory (17-19 points)**

- Limited connections to writing
- Surface understanding of style
- Knows some influences
- Significant gaps

**Needs Improvement (14-16 points)**
- Minimal biographical knowledge
- Cannot connect life to work
- Little understanding of influences
- Major gaps or errors

**Unsatisfactory (0-13 points)**
- Little to no biographical knowledge
- No understanding of influences
- Cannot discuss writing philosophy

### 3. Key Themes in the Novels (40 points)

**Excellent (36-40 points)**
- Identifies and analyzes multiple themes in each work
- Provides textual evidence for themes
- Understands how themes interconnect across works
- Can discuss Hemingway's treatment of universal themes
- Demonstrates sophisticated literary analysis

**A Moveable Feast - Major Themes:**
- **Poverty and artistic struggle**: Starving artist in Paris, sacrificing for art
- **Memory and nostalgia**: Idealized recollection of youth
- **Writing and craft**: The process, discipline, and philosophy of writing
- **Lost Generation**: Post-WWI disillusionment and expatriate culture
- **Mentorship and rivalry**: Relationships with other writers
- **Paris as character**: The city as inspiration and muse

**A Farewell to Arms - Major Themes:**
- **War and its futility**: The meaninglessness of war, random death
- **Love and loss**: Romance amid chaos; tragic love story
- **Disillusionment**: Loss of faith in institutions and ideals
- **Code hero**: Stoic masculinity, grace under pressure
- **Nature vs. civilization**: Mountains (good) vs. plains (death)
- **Individual vs. society**: Desertion, rejection of authority
- **Death and mortality**: Constant presence of death

**The Old Man and the Sea - Major Themes:**
- **Perseverance and determination**: "A man can be destroyed but not defeated"
- **Dignity in struggle**: Maintaining honor despite defeat
- **Man vs. nature**: Respect for nature's power and beauty
- **Isolation and companionship**: Solitude broken by memory and hope
- **Pride and humility**: Balance between confidence and respect
- **Youth and age**: Vitality vs. experience, Manolin vs. Santiago
- **Symbolism**: Christ figure, lions (dreams of youth)

**Recurring Hemingway Themes Across All Works:**
- The "code hero" (grace under pressure)
- Sparse, understated emotion
- Death and mortality
- Masculine identity
- Nature as teacher
- Stoicism
- Truth through simplicity

### 4. Extra Credit: Impact and Important Facts (10 points)

**Points awarded for knowledge of the novels' cultural impact and significant literary facts:**

**Excellent Knowledge (9-10 points)**
- Discusses 4+ significant impacts or facts
- Demonstrates understanding of literary and cultural influence
- Connects to broader literary movements
- Shows sophisticated understanding of legacy

**Important Facts and Impact:**

**General Hemingway Impact:**
- Revolutionized American prose style
- Influenced countless writers (minimalism, directness)
- Defined "Lost Generation" literature
- Changed war literature forever
- Popularized modernist techniques for mass audiences

**The Old Man and the Sea:**
- Won Pulitzer Prize (1953)
- Cited specifically in Nobel Prize (1954)
- Revived Hemingway's career after critical failures
- Adapted into multiple films
- Most widely taught Hemingway work
- Shortest of his major novels (novella, ~27,000 words)

**A Farewell to Arms:**
- Considered one of greatest war novels ever written
- Banned in Italy under Mussolini
- Banned in Boston for language (1929)
- Influenced subsequent war literature
- Multiple film adaptations (1932, 1957)
- Introduced "Hemingway hero" archetype

**A Moveable Feast:**
- Published posthumously (1964)
- Controversial for portrayals of famous writers
- Multiple editions with different content
- Restored edition published in 2009
- Shaped public image of 1920s Paris
- Key document of Lost Generation
- Questions about accuracy and embellishment

**Other Significant Facts:**
- Hemingway's iceberg theory: 90% beneath surface
- Influence on journalism and creative nonfiction
- Subject of scholarly debate: misogyny vs. complex female characters
- Impact on American masculinity concepts
- Influence on film (visual, sparse dialogue)
- Translation challenges due to simplicity

## Knowledge Base: Hemingway Quick Reference

### Writing Style Characteristics
- Short, declarative sentences
- Simple, concrete language
- Minimal adjectives and adverbs
- Dialogue-driven
- Objective, journalistic tone
- Omission/understatement (iceberg theory)
- Repetition for emphasis

### The "Code Hero"
- Grace under pressure
- Stoic acceptance of fate
- Physical courage
- Skill and competence
- Honesty and authenticity
- Individual code of behavior
- Examples: Santiago, Frederic Henry

### Common Symbols
- **Mountains**: Purity, safety, escape
- **Rain**: Death, tragedy
- **The sea**: Nature's power, test of man
- **Bulls**: Courage, honor, death
- **Lions**: Youth, strength, dreams

### Critical Reception Evolution
- 1920s-1940s: Revolutionary, celebrated
- 1950s: Criticized, career in decline
- *The Old Man and the Sea*: Comeback
- 1960s-1970s: Feminist criticism emerged
- Modern era: Complex reassessment
