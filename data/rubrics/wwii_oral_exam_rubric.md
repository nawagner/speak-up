## Component Breakdown

### 1. Dates, Events, and Timelines (30 points)

**Excellent (27-30 points)**
- Accurately identifies specific dates for major events (within days/weeks)
- Correctly sequences events in proper chronological order
- Understands cause-and-effect relationships between events
- Can identify turning points and their significance
- Demonstrates knowledge of both European and Pacific theaters

**Key Events to Know:**
- Invasion of Poland (September 1, 1939)
- Fall of France (June 1940)
- Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941)
- D-Day (June 6, 1944)
- V-E Day (May 8, 1945)
- Atomic bombings (August 6 & 9, 1945)
- V-J Day (August 15, 1945)

**Good (24-26 points)**
- Accurate dates for major events (within months)
- Generally correct chronology with minor errors
- Understands most cause-and-effect relationships
- Identifies several key turning points
- Knowledge of both theaters with some gaps

**Satisfactory (21-23 points)**
- Approximate dates (within seasons/years)
- Basic chronology with some confusion
- Limited understanding of causation
- Identifies 1-2 turning points
- Focuses primarily on one theater

**Needs Improvement (18-20 points)**
- Vague timeframes only
- Significant chronological errors
- Little understanding of relationships between events
- Cannot identify turning points
- Very limited theater coverage

**Unsatisfactory (0-17 points)**
- Incorrect or no dates provided
- Events completely out of sequence
- No grasp of historical progression

---

### 2. Important Figures and Their Roles (30 points)

**Excellent (27-30 points)**
- Identifies 10+ key figures with specific roles and contributions
- Explains strategic decisions and their impacts
- Understands relationships between leaders
- Covers Allied, Axis, and resistance leaders
- Can discuss lesser-known but significant figures

**Key Figures to Know:**

**Allied Leaders:**
- Winston Churchill (UK Prime Minister)
- Franklin D. Roosevelt (US President)
- Harry Truman (US President, 1945)
- Joseph Stalin (Soviet Premier)
- Charles de Gaulle (Free French leader)
- Dwight D. Eisenhower (Supreme Allied Commander)
- Douglas MacArthur (Pacific Theater Commander)
- George Patton (US General)
- Bernard Montgomery (British Field Marshal)

**Axis Leaders:**
- Adolf Hitler (German Führer)
- Benito Mussolini (Italian Duce)
- Hideki Tojo (Japanese Prime Minister)
- Hermann Göring (Luftwaffe Commander)
- Erwin Rommel (German Field Marshal)

**Other Significant Figures:**
- Oskar Schindler (industrialist who saved Jews)
- Anne Frank (Holocaust victim/diarist)
- J. Robert Oppenheimer (Manhattan Project)

**Good (24-26 points)**
- Identifies 7-9 key figures with roles
- Explains major decisions
- Understands basic relationships
- Covers Allied and Axis leaders
- Focuses on most prominent figures

**Satisfactory (21-23 points)**
- Identifies 5-6 key figures
- Basic understanding of roles
- Limited explanation of decisions
- Focuses primarily on one side
- Only most famous figures

**Needs Improvement (18-20 points)**
- Identifies 3-4 figures
- Vague understanding of roles
- Cannot explain specific contributions
- Very limited coverage
- Confuses figures and their roles

**Unsatisfactory (0-17 points)**
- Identifies fewer than 3 figures
- Incorrect information about roles
- Cannot explain any contributions

---

### 3. Key Battles and Their Strategic Importance (40 points)

**Excellent (36-40 points)**
- Detailed knowledge of 8+ major battles
- Explains strategic importance and outcomes
- Understands how battles affected war trajectory
- Covers both theaters comprehensively
- Can analyze tactical and strategic elements

**Major Battles to Know:**

**European Theater:**
- Battle of Britain (1940) - prevented German invasion of UK
- Operation Barbarossa (1941) - German invasion of USSR
- Battle of Stalingrad (1942-43) - turning point on Eastern Front
- Battle of El Alamein (1942) - turned tide in North Africa
- D-Day/Normandy Invasion (1944) - opened Western Front
- Battle of the Bulge (1944-45) - last major German offensive

**Pacific Theater:**
- Pearl Harbor (1941) - brought US into war
- Battle of Midway (1942) - turning point in Pacific
- Guadalcanal (1942-43) - first major Allied offensive
- Iwo Jima (1945) - strategic airfield for bombing Japan
- Okinawa (1945) - last major battle before atomic bombs

**Good (32-35 points)**
- Knowledge of 6-7 major battles
- Explains importance of most battles
- Understands basic strategic impact
- Covers both theaters with some gaps
- Can discuss outcomes

**Satisfactory (28-31 points)**
- Knowledge of 4-5 major battles
- Basic understanding of importance
- Limited strategic analysis
- Focuses primarily on one theater
- Describes battles but limited context

**Needs Improvement (24-27 points)**
- Knowledge of 2-3 battles
- Vague understanding of importance
- Cannot explain strategic impact
- Very limited theater coverage
- Surface-level descriptions only

**Unsatisfactory (0-23 points)**
- Knowledge of 1 or fewer battles
- No understanding of strategic importance
- Cannot explain outcomes or impacts

### 4. Extra Credit: Modern Day Connections (10 points)

**Points awarded for thoughtful connections between WWII and contemporary events/consequences:**

**Excellent Connections (9-10 points)**
- Makes 4+ sophisticated connections
- Demonstrates deep understanding of historical continuity
- Connects to current geopolitics, institutions, or conflicts
- Shows nuanced understanding of causation

**Examples:**
- Formation of United Nations and current international order
- NATO and European security architecture
- Nuclear non-proliferation treaties and current tensions
- Israeli-Palestinian conflict and Middle East politics
- Rise of China and Pacific regional dynamics
- European Union as response to nationalism
- Universal Declaration of Human Rights
- Decolonization movements and current global South
- Cold War origins and current US-Russia relations
- War crimes tribunals and International Criminal Court

**Good Connections (7-8 points)**
- Makes 3 solid connections
- Shows good understanding of consequences
- Links to current events or institutions
- Demonstrates clear causation

**Satisfactory Connections (5-6 points)**
- Makes 2 connections
- Basic understanding of legacy
- Some relevance to modern world
- Connections are somewhat superficial

**Minimal Connections (3-4 points)**
- Makes 1 connection
- Limited understanding of continuity
- Weak or forced connection
- Very general observations

**No Credit (0-2 points)**
- No connections made, or
- Connections are historically inaccurate

## Knowledge Base: Essential WWII Facts

### Timeline Overview

**1939**
- September 1: Germany invades Poland; WWII begins
- September 3: Britain and France declare war on Germany

**1940**
- May-June: Fall of France
- July-October: Battle of Britain

**1941**
- June 22: Germany invades Soviet Union
- December 7: Japan attacks Pearl Harbor; US enters war

**1942**
- June 4-7: Battle of Midway
- August: Battle of Stalingrad begins
- November: Operation Torch (North Africa)

**1943**
- February: Germans surrender at Stalingrad
- July: Battle of Kursk; Italy invaded by Allies
- September: Italy surrenders

**1944**
- June 6: D-Day invasion of Normandy
- August 25: Liberation of Paris
- December: Battle of the Bulge

**1945**
- February: Yalta Conference
- April 30: Hitler commits suicide
- May 8: V-E Day (Victory in Europe)
- August 6: Atomic bomb dropped on Hiroshima
- August 9: Atomic bomb dropped on Nagasaki
- August 15: V-J Day (Victory over Japan)
- September 2: Japan formally surrenders

### Casualties
- Total deaths: 70-85 million (approximately 3% of world population)
- Soviet Union: 24+ million
- China: 15-20 million
- Germany: 7-9 million
- Poland: 5.6 million
- Japan: 2.5-3.1 million
- Holocaust victims: 6 million Jews + millions of others
